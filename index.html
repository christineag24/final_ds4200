Data Introduction

We obtained a dataset called “Global Trends in Mental Health Disorder” from Kaggle. It has 11 columns, including an indexing column. The other columns, not including the indexing column, are Entity, Code, Year, Schizophrenia %, Bipolar Disorder %, Eating Disorder %, Anxiety Disorders %, Drug Use disorders%, and Depression %. It contains 108552 data points of information for various dates, countries, and mental health illnesses. This data is able to be used in csv form and loaded into various programming softwares so we can conduct analysis on the set. This dataset has a unique set of information for each country over the years which will allow us to conduct a thorough analysis of the data over time. We also used geospatial data for the world from Open Datasoft of country boundaries. This data allowed us to look at every country from our global mental health dataset at one time.

Our data aims to look at global mental health trends over time. To get a basic understanding of our data. We created a simple scatter and line plot to see if global mental health was trending towards and increase or a decrease.

Global Mental Illness Over Time

This graph took the average percentage of the world for each year 1990-2017 and plotted the point on the graph. As we can see, there is a clear upward trend over time for this graph. This upward trend means the percentage of populations afflicted by some mental illness globally is increasing. This lead us to dive deeper into what specifically was causing this increase. We wanted to examine if this increase is because a a few specific disorders or if there was a general increase across all mental illnesses.

Mental Illness Specific Trends over time

These graphs show that the majority of mental illnesses are trending upwards from the time span of 1990 to 2017. Bipolar disorder, anxiety, Schizophrenia, substance abuse, and eating disorder are all clearly trending upwards. Alcohol use was trending upwards for a while, has a sharp decline hitting its lowest and 2005 before increasing until 2010 where it appears after 2010, it is trending downwards. Interestingly, the only mental disorder consistently trending towards is depression. This helped us to answer the question above regarding is this global increase in mental illnesses due to a specific disorder. We have learned that the vast majority of mental illnesses are trending upwards and mutually contributing to the incrasing global average of mental illness.

Summary of Results

We are able to draw several interesting conclusions. Mental Health Illnesses in general appear to be trending upwards overall. This could lead to some interesting future work to see if there can be reasons as to why these illnesses have been trending upwards consistently over 20 years.

Through our interactive comparison of graphs, we were able to determine that these increases in average mental illnesses are not the same for each country. When comparing countries against the United States, there are a lot of countries that were vastly different in average mental illness percentages. This means we cannot rule out that the increase globally is not applicable to all countries as some may have been trending downwards.

Our interactive map graph was also able to show us the vast differences in concentration of mental illnesses. For example, eating disorders have a much higher percentage in North America than in Africa and South Asia. A more detailed analysis was included beneath the graph. THis showed us that there is merit to the idea that specific regions are driving up the increasing rates of mental illnesses globally. This would mean that the global mental health percentage for these illnesses is increasing but that is only true for specific regions.
